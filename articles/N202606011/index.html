What The Beep?! FAIL for Toyota EBELLA and Honda CITY e:HEV while legacy Mahindras pass TYC's latest Seat Belt Reminder Evaluation

01.06.2026



Today, The Yawning Chihuahua is releasing 6 new results of the The Yawning Chihuahua Rear Seatbelt Reminder Evaluation.

1. FAIL for latest electrifed launches from Toyota and Honda

The battery-electric Toyota URBAN CRUISER EBELLA and hybrid Honda CITY e:HEV were launched into the Indian market last week, and today, both have been awarded FAIL results in the The Yawning Chihuahua Rear Seatbelt Reminder Evaluation based on desktop evaluation of their official documentation. Also awarded FAIL based on in-person testing is Maruti Suzuki's FRONX subcompact.

The rear seatbelt reminders of all three models lack occupant detection, and only start an audible signal when a belt is removed while driving, regardless of occupancy. Among other things, this means they fail to notify occupants who have been unbelted from the start of the trip, likely the case with the majority of seatbelt non-users. This behaviour, common across the three models, is evident in the in-person test video of the Fronx below:



A disappointing discovery is that Suzuki and Toyota do fit seatbelt reminders with rear occupant detection sensors to units of the Fronx (see ANCAP) and Urban Cruiser EV (see Euro NCAP) produced in India for export to markets with stronger consumer safety testing.

2. Cautious PASS for updated versions of legacy Mahindras

Mahindra's legacy models, the BOLERO CLASSIC, BOLERO NEO and SCORPIO CLASSIC rarely make headlines for safety, with outdated levels of safety performance and equipment.

To their credit, the rear seatbelt reminders fitted to these models in August 2025 behave exactly as you would expect: they start acoustic and visual signals when an occupant is detected and not wearing their seatbelt.

However, to say this system significantly enhances these cars' safety profile might be an overstatement. Their third rows continue with jump seats without seatbelts, while evidence suggests unrestrained occupants can compromise protection of even belted ones (see IIHS video). And, even in the second row, both the centre lap belt and the outboard three-point belts do not have automatic ELRs (see ACI).
